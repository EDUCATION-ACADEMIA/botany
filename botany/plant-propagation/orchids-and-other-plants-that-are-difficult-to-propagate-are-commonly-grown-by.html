PLANT PROPAGATION

[SOURCES]
Orchids and other plants that are difficult to propagate are commonly grown by:

(A) budding

(B) layering

(C) seed propagation

(D) ** tissue culture

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Tissue Culture of Orchids. The cultivation of monocot explants is more difficult to culture than dicot explants. However, some parts of some species of orchids, like floral meristem and protocorms of genus Cymbidium, can be cultured using a simple vitamin B-containing medium.

Concept note-2: -, a type of micropropagation by tissue culture, is the standard method for mass propagation of the most popular orchids. the ever-popular phalaenopsis, for exam-ple, are normally propagated through tissue culture, because vegetative propagation of these orchids is slow, variable and difficult.

Concept note-3: -Plants important to developing countries that have been grown in tissue culture are oil palm, plantain, pine, banana, date, eggplant, jojoba, pineapple, rubber tree, cassava, yam, sweet potato, and tomato.

Concept note-4: -But the most commonly used media for the propagation of orchids are MS, VW and KC. In the initial stage of research only solid media were used. Later on researchers started using a liquid medium either in static or in moving conditions. After 1960 a revolution of sorts took place in the clonal propagation of orchids.

Concept note-5: -Micropropagation is the artificial process of producing plants vegetatively through tissue culture or cell culture techniques.
