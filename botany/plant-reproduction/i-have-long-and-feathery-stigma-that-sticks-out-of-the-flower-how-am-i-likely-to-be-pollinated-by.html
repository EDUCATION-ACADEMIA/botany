I have long and feathery stigma that sticks out of the flower. How am I likely to be pollinated by?

(A) ** By wind

(B) By insects

(C) By water

(D) By explosive action

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Wind-pollinated plants produce large numbers of light, dry pollen that are easier to carry by the wind. Stigma is the topmost surface of the pistil that receives pollen grains. Wind-pollinated flowers have a large, feathery stigma in order to trap the airborne pollen grains.

Concept note-2: -Insect pollinated flowers have bright-colored petals, and they have a pleasant smell. The pollen grains are lighter in weight so that they can be carried out easily. The pollen grains are larger and are sticky so that the insects can carry them with ease. These flowers have a feathery stigma with petals hanging out.

Concept note-3: -The top part of the pistil is called the stigma and is sticky so it will trap and hold pollen. In flowering plants, the flower functions in sexual reproduction. The essential flower parts are the male parts called the stamens and the female part called the pistil.

Concept note-4: -Pollination is the act of transferring pollen grains from the male anther of a flower to the female stigma.

Concept note-5: -The pollen grains are very small and light so they are easily carried on the wind. A lot of pollen is produced, which increases the chances of a pollen grain reaching the stigma in another flower. The stigmas are long and feathery, which gives a large surface area for catching pollen.
