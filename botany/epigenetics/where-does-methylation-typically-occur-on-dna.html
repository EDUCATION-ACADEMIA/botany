Where does methylation typically occur on DNA?

(A) Methylation occurs on the 3rd carbon of guanosine on a cytosine-guanosine dinucleotide

(B) Methylation occurs on the 3rd carbon of a cytosine on a cytosine-guanosine base pair

(C) Methylation occurs on the 5th carbon on a cytosine on a cytosine-guanosine base pair

(D) ** Methylation occurs on the 5th carbon on a cytosine on a cytosine-guanosine dinucleotide

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -In mammals, DNA methylation occurs at cytosines in any context of the genome.

Concept note-2: -The majority of DNA methylation occurs on cytosines that precede a guanine nucleotide or CpG sites.

Concept note-3: -Guanine residues in DNA can become methylated when exposed to alkylating agents that are either ingested in the diet or administered as antineoplastic drugs (Fig. 15-15). The methyl group on the guanine ring causes it to mispair with thymine rather than to pair with cytosine, causing a point mutation.

Concept note-4: -Molecular and genetic studies in mammals have shown that DNA cytosine methylation (abbreviated to 5mC, for 5-methyl cytosine) is associated with gene silencing. It also plays an important role in developmental processes such as X-chromosome inactivation and genomic imprinting.

Concept note-5: -5mC is the normal cytosine nucleotide in DNA that has been modified by the addition of a methyl group to its 5th carbon. The role of this mark is so distinct that many consider 5mC to be the “5th base” of DNA. This methylation typically occurs at cytosine in CpG dinucleotides in vertebrates.
