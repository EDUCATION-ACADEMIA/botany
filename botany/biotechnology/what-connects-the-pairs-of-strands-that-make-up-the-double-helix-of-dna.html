What connects the pairs of strands that make up the double helix of DNA?

(A) ** Bases

(B) Starches

(C) Sugars

(D) Tar

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Molecules called nucleotides, on opposite strands of the DNA double helix, that form chemical bonds with one another. These chemical bonds act like rungs in a ladder and help hold the two strands of DNA together.

Concept note-2: -Each molecule of DNA is a double helix formed from two complementary strands of nucleotides held together by hydrogen bonds between G-C and A-T base pairs.

Concept note-3: -The chemical backbones of the double helix are made up of sugar and phosphate molecules that are connected by chemical bonds, known as sugar-phosphate backbones. The two helical strands are connected through interactions between pairs of nucleotides, also called base pairs.

Concept note-4: -The two DNA strands are held together by hydrogen (H) bonds formed between the bases adenine and thymine to form the AT base pair and between the bases guanine and cytosine to form the GC base pair. Watson and Crick published the double helical structure of DNA in 1953 (Figure 1(b)).

Concept note-5: -The bases of one strand bond to the bases of the second strand with hydrogen bonds. Adenine always bonds with thymine, and cytosine always bonds with guanine. The bonding causes the two strands to spiral around each other in a shape called a double helix.
