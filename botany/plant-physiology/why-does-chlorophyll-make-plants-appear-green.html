Why does chlorophyll make plants appear green?

(A) Reflecting all colors expect green

(B) ** Absorbing all colors and reflecting back green

(C) Not absorbing any colors

(D) Leaves aren’t green, what do you mean?!

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -As shown in detail in the absorption spectra, chlorophyll absorbs light in the red (long wavelength) and the blue (short wavelength) regions of the visible light spectrum. Green light is not absorbed but reflected, making the plant appear green.

Concept note-2: -So, plants and their leaves look green because the “special pair” of chlorophyll molecules uses the red end of the visible light spectrum to power reactions inside each cell. The unused green light is reflected from the leaf and we see that light.

Concept note-3: -The simple answer is that although plants absorb almost all the photons in the red and blue regions of the light spectrum, they absorb only about 90% of the green photons. If they absorbed more, they would look black to our eyes. Plants are green because the small amount of light they reflect is that color.

Concept note-4: -Chlorophyll is a magnesium porphyrin complex present in of green plants. When chlorophyll is exposed to light, it absorbs all the wavelengths except green therefore in reflected light it appears green and that is why all plants are green. Chlorophyll appears red in transmitted light because of fluorescence.

Concept note-5: -In conclusion, plant leaves are green because green light is less efficiently absorbed by chlorophylls a and b than red or blue light, and therefore green light has a higher probability to become diffusely reflected from cell walls than red or blue light.
